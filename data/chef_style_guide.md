# Chef's Style Guide

This document outlines the culinary philosophy, menu structure, ingredient preferences, and textural/flavor balancing principles observed across a collection of menus. This guide aims to capture the chef's signature style for automated menu generation.

## 1. Overall Culinary Philosophy

*   **Modern European with Mediterranean & Italian Influences:** The chef exhibits a clear affinity for modern European cuisine, heavily influenced by Mediterranean and particularly Italian flavors. Dishes showcase a blend of classic techniques with contemporary presentations.
*   **Ingredient-Driven:** Emphasis is placed on high-quality, seasonal ingredients. The menus frequently mention sourcing and preparation methods that highlight freshness and natural flavors.
*   **Sophisticated Simplicity:** While some dishes involve complex preparations, the overall goal seems to be showcasing the inherent qualities of the ingredients. The style tends to favor clean, well-defined flavors over overly complicated combinations.
*   **Global Influences (subtle):** While European cuisine forms the core, there are instances of Asian flavors (miso, yuzu, matcha, Thai vinaigrette) and Latin American dishes (ceviche, aguachile, tacos) integrated to add depth and complexity. These elements are typically used sparingly and thoughtfully.
*   **Visually Appealing:** The descriptions often imply a focus on aesthetic presentation, with attention paid to color, texture, and plating.

## 2. Menu Structure

*   **Variable Course Count:** Menus range from 3 to 8 courses, and even larger for event-style menus like the Mexican-themed one. This suggests adaptability based on client preferences and event type.
*   **Amuse-Bouche/Snacks:** Amuse-bouche or small snacks are common in longer menus, setting the tone for the meal and offering a preview of the chef's style. These are often creative and bite-sized, highlighting premium ingredients.
*   **Progression:**
    *   **Starters/Appetizers:** Typically feature lighter, brighter flavors to awaken the palate. Crudos, tartares, salads, and vegetable-focused dishes are frequently used.
    *   **Pasta/Risotto Course:** A pasta or risotto course is a common element in longer menus, reflecting the Italian influence. These dishes often showcase rich sauces and seasonal ingredients.
    *   **Main Course(s):** Usually features a protein-centric dish, often roasted or pan-seared. Sauces are carefully considered and complement the protein without overpowering it.
    *   **Cheese Course (Rare):** Occasionally, a cheese course is incorporated as a transition between the main course and dessert.
    *   **Dessert:** Desserts tend to be classic with a modern twist, often incorporating seasonal fruits and sophisticated flavor combinations. Textural contrast is also common.
*   **Drinks:** The inclusion of "Mezcalitas" in one menu suggests the ability to incorporate themed beverages.

## 3. Ingredient Preferences

*   **Proteins:**
    *   **Seafood:** Sériole (Yellowtail), Tuna (often spicy), Saint Jacques (Scallops), Sole, Turbot, Maigre, Gambas (Red Prawns), Lobster, Cod, Sardines, and various shellfish are frequently used. Often served raw or lightly cooked.
    *   **Meat:** Veal, Lamb, Beef (especially Ribeye, Tournedos, and matured beef), Pork (Côte de Porc, Iberico), Duck, and Game (Venison, Chevreuil, Faisan, Sanglier) are all prominent.
    *   **Poultry:** Quail (Scotch Eggs), Chicken (often in satay form), Faraona (Guinea Fowl).
*   **Premium/Luxury Ingredients:**
    *   **Truffles:** Black and white truffles are used extensively, often shaved over pasta, risotto, or meat dishes.
    *   **Caviar:** Frequently paired with seafood or used as a garnish to add a touch of elegance. Ikura (Salmon Roe) also appears regularly.
    *   **Foie Gras:** Used in terrines, Wellington, or as an accompaniment to meat.
    *   **Guanciale:** Cured pork cheek, a staple in Italian cuisine, adds richness and savory depth.
    *   **Burrata:** Fresh Italian cheese, used in appetizers and salads.
    *   **Poutargue/Bottarga:** Cured fish roe, used to add a briny, umami flavor to pasta dishes.
    *   **Saffron:** Used in risotto and sauces.
    *   **Parmesan/Comté/Pecorino:** Premium cheeses, often aged.
*   **Vegetables:**
    *   Artichokes (confit, fried, puréed)
    *   Asparagus (green, white)
    *   Peas (petits pois, fèves)
    *   Tomatoes (various types, often heirloom)
    *   Courgettes/Zucchini (flowers, roasted)
    *   Potatoes (various preparations: Boulangères, Feuilleté, Purée)
    *   Mushrooms (Ceps, Girolles, Chanterelles, Porcini)
    *   Topinambour (Jerusalem Artichoke)
    *   Beets
    *   Pumpkin/Squash
*   **Flavor Pairings:**
    *   Seafood & Citrus (lemon, lime, yuzu, clementine)
    *   Truffle & Egg (raviolo with confit egg yolk)
    *   Pork & Fruit (mirabelles, prunes)
    *   Chocolate & Pistachio/Orange/Bergamot
    *   Lamb & Mint/Salsa Verde
    *   Veal & Mustard
    *   Beef & Bordelaise Sauce
*   **Herbs & Spices:**
    *   Basil
    *   Mint
    *   Cresson
    *   Chives
    *   Sauge (Sage)
    *   Aneth (Dill)
    *   Romarin (Rosemary)
    *   Ail des Ours (Ramsons/Wild Garlic)
    *   Poivre (Pepper)
    *   Pimenté (Spicy)

## 4. Textural & Balancing Rules

*   **Contrast is Key:** Dishes often incorporate contrasting textures to create a more dynamic eating experience. Examples include:
    *   Crispy elements (crispy shallots, pommes allumettes, crispy polenta, polenta croustillante, artichaut croustillant, rice) paired with creamy elements (espumas, sauces).
    *   Tender proteins paired with crunchy vegetables or garnishes.
    *   Smooth purées juxtaposed with al dente pasta.
*   **Acidic Balance:** Acidity is used to cut through richness and balance flavors. Citrus fruits, vinegars, and pickled vegetables are frequently employed.
*   **Rich vs. Light:** Menus are carefully constructed to balance rich and light dishes. For instance, a heavier pasta course might be followed by a lighter protein dish with a vibrant sauce.
*   **Sweet & Savory:** The chef is comfortable with incorporating sweet elements into savory dishes, such as fruit glazes for pork or honey-roasted vegetables. However, sweetness is typically balanced with acidity or spice.
*   **Umami Emphasis:** Umami-rich ingredients like truffles, mushrooms, parmesan, miso, and seaweed are used to add depth and complexity to dishes.
